Ethical Dilemma Analysis

Insider Misuse of Administrative Privileges (The 2020 Twitter Security Incident) [cite: 115, 118]

The 2020 Twitter security incident stands out as a classic ethical dilemma regarding administrative trust[cite: 118]. During this breach, external threat actors utilized social engineering—specifically targeting human vulnerabilities rather than network vulnerabilities—to compromise internal employees who held high-level administrative credentials[cite: 119]. With access to these internal administration panels, the attackers gained complete control over verified, high-profile accounts (including business leaders and public figures) to spread a fraudulent cryptocurrency scam[cite: 120].

This scenario represents a serious violation of ethical rules and administrative security[cite: 116]. System administrators are granted "the keys to the castle" and hold an implicit covenant to use their elevated privileges responsibly to protect user privacy and system integrity[cite: 117]. When employees failed to recognize social engineering tactics, they unintentionally exposed administrative channels[cite: 119].

Twitter’s initial response included locking down the affected accounts, deleting the scam posts, conducting a forensic investigation, and cooperating with global law enforcement[cite: 124]. To mitigate future insider risks, they restricted administrative access and retrained employees on security awareness[cite: 125]. This case demonstrates that technical tools alone cannot protect a network without ethical stewardship and strong training[cite: 127, 128]. This reinforces my desire to be a security professional whom employers and users can trust completely with sensitive administrative control[cite: 129].
